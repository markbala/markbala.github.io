Assembling a 3d Printer

Assembling a 3d Printer

  • Mar 14 2017//
  • 1 comment

  • People: Alex Lim, Mark Bala
  • Date: End 2016

    • I've always wondered how 3d printers work. I figured assembling one would help me to better understand the mechanics behind it. After a week of research, reading reviews, and asking for advice, we settled on the FLSUN Kossel Delta 3d Printer. It took a week to ship from China and set us back roughly US$250. We assembled it in approximately 8 hours, split over 2 evenings.

    Understanding the printer:
  • Our printer consists of an aluminum frame, held together by plastic joints.
  • Literally everything that isn't metal can be printed (replicated) by the 3d printer.
  • The frame is in the shape of a triangular prism.
  • 3 sets of carbon fibre rods connect the extruder head to the frame and form the shape of a triangle, hence the term 'delta'.
  • The rods are attached to rolling bearings that move freely up and down each aluminum rod.
  • 3 stepper motors are hidden under each of the vertical rods, and they work together to move the rods (and hence the extruder head) along the x,y,z axes.
  • Computing hardware: The printer is powered by a MEGA 2560 chip which is hidden underneath the printer bed
  • Software: We are using Slic3r and Repetier Host on a Windows computer.
  • After downloading or designing a 3d model (STL file), slic3r is used to process the model.
  • It turns the model into a long list of x,y co-ordinates that tell the printer how it should move within each layer so as to draw the object.
  • After a layer is complete, the printer head moves up (z-axis) by a specific amount, and follows the co-ordinates as defined in the next layer
  • A 4th stepper motor lives at the top of the printer, and its sole function is to push filament ('extrude') into the extruder head, or pull filament out ('retract')
  • This is essentially a "print" / "do not print" function.
  • Combined with the x,y co-ordinates, the printer draws each layer accurately, making sure not to print over spaces that should be blank.
  • Everything can be customised. Print speed, layer thickness, infill density and pattern, temperature, retraction length and speed, support structures and rafts, cooling techniques, first layer specifics, etc.

    • Learning Points:
  • Every variable matters, and variables are often interdependent, affecting and interacting with each other.
  • Calibration is an art.
  • Consider printing orientation (i.e. whether to print an object as-is, on its side, or upside down).
  • Every problem we encountered had been experienced by someone else and solutions are readily available online.

    • Ideas for development:
  • Our specific printer model is a commonly modified printer.
  • Many parts have been redesigned and can be printed and replaced (i.e. fan, damper, tensioner, auto-level).
  • Some of these modifications improve print quality. Others make the printer run quieter.

    • Mistakes:
  • We had to cut and re-solder some wires so as to avoid taking apart hours of work.
  • We clogged the extruder after a month of use.
  • Printing models that are too large will cause the extruder to crash into the aluminum rods.

    • Things we are happy with:
  • We can make stuff now.
  • It is cheap to print (one kg of PLA filament is about $8-12 from Taobao).
  • Our printer is fairly well calibrated- a 2cm model becomes a 2cm print.
  • Although for the most time, we just printed frivolous objects, along the way we've also made some things that are useful for work and daily life such as a tap key, christmas decorations, bottle opener and a tablet stand.
  • We've learnt the mechanics of a delta 3d printer.
  • We've gotten a decent grasp on the nuances and pitfalls in calibration.
